# Target-Date Retirement Fund

## What it is
A target-date fund (TDF) is a single mutual fund or ETF that holds a globally
diversified mix of stocks and bonds, with the allocation automatically becoming
more conservative as the target retirement year approaches (the "glidepath").
Vanguard, Fidelity, and Schwab each offer TDF lineups with extremely low costs.
A 35-year-old picks something like Vanguard Target Retirement 2055 (VFFVX);
the fund manager handles all rebalancing and asset-allocation drift.

## When it works
- **Hands-off retirement saving**: ideal for 401(k) defaults and IRAs.
- **Behavioral protection**: removes the temptation to tinker with allocation.
- **Tax-advantaged accounts**: optimal home is IRA / 401(k) / Roth where the
  capital-gains distributions don't matter.

## When it fails
- **Glidepath mismatch**: a 30-year-old with high risk tolerance may be
  under-allocated to equities. A 65-year-old who plans to work to 75 may be
  over-allocated to bonds.
- **2022 stress**: bonds and stocks fell together. TDFs targeting 2025-2030
  drew down 15-20% — surprising for "near-retirement" funds.
- **Tax drag in taxable accounts**: TDFs distribute capital gains even if you
  don't sell. This is a known weakness vs. holding the underlying ETFs
  directly. Bogleheads consistently flags this issue.
- **No tax-loss harvesting**: you cannot harvest the equity sleeve's losses
  because the fund is one ticker.

## Tax & compliance considerations
- In an IRA / 401(k): no tax friction; this is the ideal home.
- In a taxable account: expect annual capital-gains distributions even if you
  do nothing. Vanguard's 2021 Target Retirement debacle distributed
  unexpectedly large gains and triggered a class-action lawsuit.
- Holdings inside the fund are not taxed by you directly — only the fund-level
  distributions are.

## $10k feasibility
Highly feasible. Vanguard mutual fund versions have a $1,000 minimum;
iShares LifePath ETFs (e.g., ITDE) have no minimum and trade like stocks.

## First-week starter checklist
1. Inside your Roth IRA (or Traditional IRA), pick a target year close to
   age 65.
2. Buy the corresponding TDF with 100% of your contribution.
3. Set up an automatic monthly contribution.
4. Confirm the fund's expense ratio is below 0.20%. (Vanguard: ~0.08%;
   Fidelity Freedom Index: ~0.12%.)
5. Do not buy in a taxable account if you have IRA space available.
