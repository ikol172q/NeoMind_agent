# Low-Volatility Factor ETF (USMV / SPLV)

## What it is
Low-volatility ETFs hold stocks with the lowest realized volatility (and/or
lowest beta) in the universe. iShares USMV uses a min-variance optimization
across MSCI USA constituents; Invesco SPLV simply holds the 100 lowest-vol
S&P 500 names equal-weighted. The factor anomaly: low-vol stocks have
historically delivered comparable returns to the market with materially less
volatility — a violation of the simple CAPM relationship.

## When it works
- **Choppy / late-cycle markets**: 2015, 2018 Q4, 2022 — low-vol outperforms.
- **Bear markets**: smaller drawdowns (USMV drew down ~21% vs S&P's 34% in
  2020).
- **Risk-adjusted returns**: Sharpe ratio is the selling point, not raw CAGR.

## When it fails
- **Risk-on rallies**: 2020 H2 and 2023 AI rally — USMV lagged by 5-10%.
- **Rate-sensitive composition**: heavy in utilities/staples/REITs makes
  low-vol look like a duration trade. 2022 hurt this composition until
  defensives reasserted late in the year.
- **Crowding**: factor degraded post-2017 with massive ETF inflows.
- **Drift**: USMV's holdings can change materially after rebalancing — your
  defensive sleeve may be less defensive than expected.

## Tax & compliance considerations
- Standard equity ETF treatment. Hold >12 months for LTCG.
- Qualified dividends prevail; ordinary income only on REIT-component
  distributions.
- Wash-sale: USMV vs SPLV may be similar enough that the IRS could call them
  "substantially identical" if you swap at a loss. No clear ruling.
- IRS Pub 550 governs holding-period and dividend rules.

## $10k feasibility
Trivial. $1,000-2,000 as a defensive sleeve.

## First-week starter checklist
1. Decide: USMV (multi-factor min-variance, lower expense) or SPLV
   (concentrated, equal-weight).
2. Buy $1,500 as a defensive tilt alongside core (VTI).
3. Evaluate over a full cycle (~5 years), not quarter-by-quarter.
4. Read the methodology document — understand how holdings are picked.
